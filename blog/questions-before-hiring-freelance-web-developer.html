10 Questions to Ask Before You Hire a Freelance Web Developer

✍️ By Alizaib📅 2025⏱ 5 min read

Most people who have bad experiences with freelancers skipped the vetting process. They saw a reasonable price, liked the portfolio, and hired — without asking the questions that actually reveal whether someone will deliver.

I'm Alizaib, a full stack freelance web developer. Here are the 10 questions I'd want a client to ask me — and why the answers matter.

The 10 Questions

01

Have you built something similar to what I need?

Relevant experience matters far more than general skill. A developer who has built 10 Telegram bots will outperform someone building their first one, regardless of how talented they are.

02

Can I see reviews from your previous clients?

Portfolio pieces can be fabricated or helped by others. Verified client reviews on platforms like Fiverr are much harder to fake. Look for specifics — "delivered on time," "great communication," "came back for another project."

03

What questions do you have about my project?

A good developer won't quote you without asking questions first. If they give you a price in 2 minutes flat, they either didn't read your request or are telling you what you want to hear.

04

Who will actually be doing the work?

Some freelancers outsource to cheaper developers and pocket the difference. Ask directly. For agencies, ask who on the team will be assigned to your project and what their experience level is.

05

Will I receive the full source code after delivery?

You should own everything. Full source code, documentation, database schema — no lock-in, no hidden dependencies. If a developer hesitates here, that's a red flag.

06

What happens if something breaks after delivery?

Every project has post-launch issues. Ask what's included in terms of support, for how long, and what costs extra. A professional freelance web developer will have a clear answer.

07

How do you handle scope changes?

Requirements change. How a developer manages this tells you a lot about how the project will go. Look for a clear, fair process — not "no changes allowed" or "I'll just add it for free no problem."

08

How will we communicate during the project?

Daily? Weekly? Via email, WhatsApp, or Telegram? Knowing upfront prevents the frustration of radio silence during a critical build phase. Good developers set clear communication expectations early.

09

What's your realistic timeline — not your best-case timeline?

Most timeline issues come from developers quoting their best-case scenario. Ask specifically: "What could delay this, and by how much?" A developer who gives you an honest answer with potential risks is being professional.

10

Can we start with a small paid test task?

For large projects, this is one of the best risk-reduction strategies available. A 4–8 hour paid task lets you evaluate the developer's code quality, communication, and reliability before committing to a larger engagement.
